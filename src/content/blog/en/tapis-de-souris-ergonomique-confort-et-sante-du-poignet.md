## Why the wrist suffers at the office - and how the pad can help

### Hand posture: an often-ignored problem

When you use a mouse without a wrist rest, your hand sits on the desk at an angle called *dorsiflexion* - meaning the wrist is raised upward. Held for several hours, this angle compresses the median nerve and the flexor tendons. According to ergonomic studies, **dorsiflexion above 15 degrees** significantly increases the risk of repetitive strain injuries (*RSI*).

A mouse pad with an integrated wrist rest helps maintain the wrist in a **neutral position**, meaning in the natural extension of the forearm. It's this position that reduces mechanical strain on the joints.

### The role of cushioning

Not all wrist rests are equal. Cushioning that's too firm creates localized pressure points on blood vessels. Cushioning that's too soft doesn't support the wrist enough. **Memory foam** remains the most popular solution, because it evenly distributes the hand's weight while absorbing repetitive micro-movements.

> A gel wrist rest can feel comfortable on first contact, but it tends to harden over time and create localized pressure under the wrist - memory foam generally lasts longer.

## Technical criteria for picking the right ergonomic pad

### Wrist-rest height and profile

The ideal wrist-rest height depends on your mouse's thickness. If the mouse base is tall, the wrist rest must be thicker to compensate. Generally, **a height between 15 and 25 mm** suits most setups. At [BuddyPad](/en/), we recommend measuring your mouse's height at the palm rest to find the right profile.

### The glide surface

An ergonomic pad must also be technically performant. For office use, a **medium-grain cloth surface** offers a good compromise between precision and fluidity. For gaming, a *speed* surface (smoother) reduces movement resistance, which decreases repetitive muscular effort on the wrist. If you're still unsure about format or material, our article [how to choose your mouse pad](/en/choisir-tapis-souris-2026/) details every option with concrete use cases.

### Size and stability

A pad that's too small forces you to constantly shift your forearm, which cancels the ergonomic effect. Prioritize **a surface of at least 300 x 250 mm (12x10 in)** for office use, and an *XXL* format if your setup also includes a keyboard. The non-slip base is essential: a pad that slides causes involuntary corrections, a source of additional tension.

## Ergonomic pad in remote work: specific challenges

Remote work has transformed our habits: we sometimes work on the corner of a table, with no real dedicated desk, with a cheap mouse and zero ergonomic accessories. This context worsens RSI risks. Setting up your home workspace isn't just about buying a chair - the input station counts just as much. Cloth remains most common, but if you often work with slightly clammy hands or in a warm environment, a **PU leather** pad or a hard surface can be more hygienic and easier to maintain.
