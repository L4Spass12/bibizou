## Why does your wrist suffer in front of a screen?

### The mechanics of the problem

When you use a mouse without proper support, your wrist rests directly on the edge of a desk or an ordinary pad. This position compresses the **carpal tunnel**, a narrow passage through which the median nerve and tendons pass. Over time, this repeated compression can cause carpal tunnel syndrome, a condition now recognized as an occupational illness in many countries.

A simple posture change isn't always enough. What's needed is a support point that **keeps the wrist slightly elevated** and in the natural axis of the forearm, so the median nerve isn't crushed.

### The intensive gaming case

Players are particularly exposed. Sessions of **4 to 6 hours** in a row, with fast and repetitive movements, multiply the risk of microtrauma. We see it on [our blog](/en/blog/): questions about wrist pain after gaming sessions come up very regularly in reader comments and searches.

> A well-designed wrist rest can reduce pressure on the median nerve by nearly 30% according to some ergonomic studies.

## The different types of wrist rests: which to choose?

### Foam, gel or cloth?

The market mainly offers three cushioning materials:

- **Memory foam** - It progressively adapts to your wrist's morphology and offers consistent support. Ideal for long office use.
- **Gel** - Firmer and cooler to the touch, gel is often preferred by gamers seeking precise support without excessive sinking.
- **Thick cloth** - Less technical, but pleasant for short sessions or a tight budget.

To go further on material and dimension choice, see our full guide [how to choose your mouse pad](/en/choisir-tapis-souris-2026/): it covers all the essential criteria so you don't make a mistake.

### Built-in wrist rest or separate accessory?

Some pads offer a **built-in wrist rest**, generally as a cushioned roll on the lower edge of the pad. It's the most practical solution: a single glide surface, a single accessory to place. Others prefer a **separate pad**, freely positionable, which can also serve in front of the keyboard. Both approaches are valid; it all depends on your desk configuration and how you work.

## The criteria of a good ergonomic wrist-rest mouse pad

### Roll height and firmness

This is the most crucial point. A wrist rest that's too tall forces the wrist into hyperextension, which is just as harmful as no support at all. The ideal height is generally between **15 and 20 mm**, depending on your hand size and mouse height. Firmness must be enough not to fully compress under the wrist's weight, without being rigid to the point of creating a localized pressure point.

### Glide surface and stability

An ergonomic pad mustn't sacrifice *glide* quality to comfort. Check that the surface stays smooth and even at the junction with the roll. The non-slip base is also essential: a pad that slides on the desk negates all ergonomic benefits. ### Format and compatibility with your setup

If you have a **large desk** or a multi-screen setup, an XXL pad with built-in wrist rest can cover mouse and keyboard on a single surface, for perfect visual and functional coherence. If your space is more constrained, a compact model with a side roll will suffice. ## Our usage tips to maximize comfort

Having the right pad isn't enough if you use it wrong. Here are the **best practices** to adopt immediately:

- Rest your **wrist, not your palm**, on the wrist rest during pauses, not during active movement.
- Keep your forearm in the **extension of your hand**, without lateral deviation.
- Take **breaks every 45 minutes** - raise your arms, stretch your wrists, rotate them.
- Adjust your chair height so your elbows are at **90°** to the desk.

> The golden rule: the wrist rest is for resting, not action. Lift your wrist when you move the mouse, place it down only when you take a pause.
